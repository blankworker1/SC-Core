The Jacket Protocol — Version 1.0

The Jacket Protocol
v1.0

Proof of Work / Wear
A ceremonial interface for making decentralised value socially visible, materially embodied, and ritually accountable.
Built to be Worn

This document governs the existence, possession, transfer, and civic function of the twenty-one jackets that constitute the Entropy Collection. Each jacket is a physical object and a digital interface simultaneously. It accumulates material evidence of its own history — wear, patches, marks, repair — while remaining linked to a permanent Bitcoin wallet and a civic record. Neither the physical object nor the digital record is primary. They are the same thing expressed in two registers.

No jacket may be owned. Each may only be possessed, for a defined period, by a custodian who accepts the obligations set out here.

To wear is to account · To scan is to witness
Part I

The Object

1.1 — The Garment

The Collection comprises exactly twenty-one jackets. This number is fixed and permanent. No new jackets may be commissioned after the system's founding. If a jacket is destroyed beyond repair, its number retires. The collection diminishes; it does not grow.

Each jacket is assigned a number at fabrication: Jacket 01 through Jacket 21. This number is permanently woven into the lining and encoded into the jacket's digital identity. It cannot be transferred or reassigned.

1.2 — Material Construction

Each suit is a ceremonial open-front waistcoat. The base garment is identical across all twenty-one at the moment of creation. Divergence begins at first wear.

Fixed elements — permanent, non-alterable:

  • Jacket number, woven into the lining
  • NFC tags embedded in four of the five buttons
  • QR code panel, woven or embroidered into the centre back
  • A founding date, stitched inside the collar

Accumulating elements — added by custodians over time:

  • Patches — added at the custodian's discretion, in any position except the QR panel
  • Embroidered marks — dates, symbols, civic insignia
  • Wear and repair — fraying, darning, reinforcement
  • Lining inscriptions — each custodian may add their name and dates of possession

No custodian may remove material added by a previous custodian. The jacket accumulates forward only.

1.3 — The QR Panel

The QR code is embroidered or woven into the centre back panel. It resolves to the jacket's permanent dashboard URL — a stable endpoint that does not change for the life of the jacket.

The panel is physically unobstructed when the jacket is worn. A custodian who wishes to suspend public scanning may cover the panel with a garment or turn away from public space. There is no electronic switch. Privacy is enacted through the body.

The QR panel is the only element of the jacket that may not be patched over, embroidered upon, or otherwise obscured by any permanent addition.

1.4 — The NFC Button System

The jacket carries five buttons in total. Four contain embedded NFC tags. One is uncoded — a plain button with no tag, present at fabrication and left deliberately unassigned.

The physical grammar of the transfer ceremony follows from this layout. The outgoing custodian reaches inside to tap the inner pocket button — a private, inward gesture. The incoming custodian taps the breast pocket button openly, in front of witnesses.

The spare uncoded button is a material acknowledgement that the system is not finished — that future custodians may, by collective amendment, assign it a purpose not yet imagined. It is the one component of the jacket that belongs entirely to the future.

1.5 — The Bag and Protocol Card

Each jacket is accompanied by a small bag, attached to the garment at fabrication. The bag contains a printed card bearing a QR code that resolves to this document.

The card serves a specific purpose: it explains the jacket to someone who encounters it without context. A person in a public space who sees the jacket and scans a button needs a way to understand what they are looking at. This document is that explanation.

The bag and card transfer with the jacket at every change of custody. If the card is lost or damaged, the custodian is responsible for printing and replacing it before transfer. The URL is permanent. The card is replaceable.

The bag carries the key to the object. The object carries the key to the system.

1.6 — The Node

Each jacket is accompanied by a node — a small, purpose-built device that runs the jacket's ledger software, stores the chain, and serves the dashboard. Raspberry Pi Zero W hardware. Custom numbered enclosure. Single LED: green for synced. One USB cable for power. One microSD card that contains everything.

The node and the jacket are a keyring. Two objects. One identity. They travel together through every custody.

The node's identity resides in the microSD card, not the enclosure. If the hardware fails, flash a new card from the open source image and the node continues. The enclosure is replaceable. The chain is not.

Part II

The Digital Layer

2.1 — The Bitcoin Wallet

Each jacket is associated with a Bitcoin extended public key (xpub), derived at fabrication. No private key is generated. This is not a security measure — it is a foundational condition. The corresponding private key does not exist, has never existed, and cannot be constructed. The Bitcoin held is permanently and irrecoverably committed.

The xpub is fully public. Anyone who scans the QR code or taps the NFC buttons receives everything necessary to derive child addresses and send Bitcoin to the jacket. Contributing is an open act.

The jacket holds what it holds. No one can take it. No one can return it. The commitment is the point.

The balance moves in one direction only: upward. Sending Bitcoin to a jacket wallet is closer in nature to a ceremonial act than a financial one.

2.2 — The Dashboard

Each jacket's dashboard is a permanent, publicly accessible webpage served by the jacket's node. It displays: current Bitcoin balance in BTC and local fiat equivalent; full custodian history; patch and mark log with photographs; ceremony record; wallet contribution history; and jacket age.

The dashboard is read-only for the public. Only the current custodian, via authenticated NFC tap, may add entries to the civic record. It is a certificate of provenance, a financial instrument display, and a civic biography simultaneously.

2.3 — Privacy

Stated plainly on every dashboard, and here:

  • No record is kept of who scans the QR code.
  • No record is kept of who taps the NFC buttons.
  • No interaction generates an identity record for the viewer.
  • The only person whose identity is recorded is the current custodian, who consents at the moment of acceptance.

These are technical commitments, not policy statements. The system is built without logging infrastructure for scan or tap events. There are no rewards for scanning. Scanning is an act of witness. Witness is its own purpose.

2.4 — The Node Network

The twenty-one nodes form a peer-to-peer network. Each holds a complete copy of every jacket's chain. There is no central server. There is no master node. Every node is equal.

As long as one node remains online, the full history of all twenty-one jackets is preserved and publicly accessible. The dashboard served from any node is identical. The network is the custodians.

Part III

Possession

3.1 — The Wearer Instrument

A traditional bearer instrument assigns rights and value to whoever physically holds it — anonymously, without registration, without intermediary. Possession is the claim. The jacket operates on the same principle, transposed to the body.

Whoever wears the jacket is the instrument. Not its owner — there are no owners. Not its representative — there is no institution to represent. The wearer, for the duration of wearing, embodies the jacket's full identity: its Bitcoin value, its civic history, its accumulated material record.

Possession is identity. Identity is value.

This is why transfer is a physical act — jacket moving from one body to another, in public, witnessed. The instrument does not transfer through paperwork or signature. It transfers through presence.

3.2 — The Nature of Possession

No person may own a jacket. Ownership implies permanent title. No such title exists in this system.

Possession is a temporary stewardship. A custodian holds the jacket for a defined period, fulfils the obligations of that period, and transfers it according to the rules below. The jacket belongs to the system. The system belongs to no one.

3.3 — Term of Possession

The standard term is one calendar year, running from the date of the annual transfer ceremony. A custodian may hold for no more than two consecutive terms. After a gap of at least one full term, a former custodian may hold again.

3.4 — Transfer Mechanism

Transfer of possession is determined by the following procedure, conducted at the annual ceremony:

  1. The outgoing custodian presents the jacket, worn, at the ceremony.
  2. The jacket's dashboard is opened to the public for the duration of the ceremony.
  3. A transfer pool is assembled: any person who has made a public declaration of intent to possess the jacket during the preceding month is eligible.
  4. From the transfer pool, the next custodian is selected by lot. Random selection is the method. It is not modified by wealth, reputation, or previous contribution.
  5. The selected person may accept or decline. If they decline, the lot is drawn again.
  6. Acceptance is spoken aloud at the ceremony, in the presence of witnesses, then logged to the civic record via NFC tap.
  7. The jacket is transferred physically, from the outgoing custodian's body to the incoming custodian's body, at the ceremony.
  8. The node — enclosure and microSD — is transferred alongside the jacket.

There is no bidding. There is no appointment. Selection is random among those who have declared intent. Declaration is the only prerequisite.

3.5 — Custodian Obligations

A custodian accepts the following at the moment of transfer:

  • To wear the jacket at least once in public during their term.
  • To present the jacket at the annual ceremony, worn and intact.
  • To photograph and log any patches or marks added during their term.
  • To maintain the physical integrity of the jacket — repairs are encouraged, destruction is forbidden.
  • To make no permanent alteration to the QR panel or NFC buttons.
  • To transfer the jacket in no worse physical condition than received, absent normal wear.
  • To keep the node powered and connected where reasonably possible.
  • To transfer the node alongside the jacket at the end of their term.
Part IV

The Annual Ceremony

4.1 — Purpose

The annual ceremony is the system's heartbeat. It is the moment at which the digital layer and the physical layer are made simultaneously visible in public space. It is not a celebration. It is an audit.

4.2 — Structure

Phase 1 — Presentation. All available jackets are worn by their current custodians in a shared public space. Dashboards are opened. The aggregate Bitcoin value of all active jackets is displayed or announced. The public may scan and tap freely.

Phase 2 — Transfer. Jackets whose term has ended undergo transfer as described in section 3.4. Each transfer is conducted publicly, spoken aloud, and logged in real time.

Phase 3 — Record. Each custodian adds a ceremony entry to their jacket's civic record. Photographs are taken. The patch and mark logs are updated.

4.3 — Location and Attendance

The ceremony is held in public civic space — a square, a market, a street, a common. It is not held in a gallery, an institution, or a private venue.

There is no guest list. The ceremony is open to anyone present in the space. Members of the public who witness a transfer become part of that transfer's civic record, not by name, but by presence. The ceremony is witnessed because it happens in public, not because witnesses have been invited.

Part V

Value

5.1 — What the System Measures

The system measures one form of value: Bitcoin held in the jacket's xpub wallet. This is deliberate. The system does not measure reputation, contribution, social metrics, attendance, or civic virtue. These things may be recorded in the civic record as narrative, but they are not quantified. The dashboard shows Bitcoin. Everything else is history.

5.2 — The Three Symmetries

The system is structurally isomorphic to Bitcoin in three independent ways. These are not references or homages. They are parallel instantiations of the same design logic in a different medium.

Supply cap. Bitcoin's supply is hard-limited to 21 million BTC. This system is hard-limited to 21 jackets. Scarcity is structural, not editorial.

Proof of work. Bitcoin's value is secured by computational proof of work: the irreversible expenditure of energy to validate the chain. The jacket's value is secured by material proof of work: the irreversible expenditure of labour in fabrication, embroidery, and accumulated craft. Same phrase. Two forms of labour. One object that holds both.

The founder's disappearance. Satoshi Nakamoto created Bitcoin and withdrew — permanently and completely — leaving a system that runs without its creator. The founding body of this system enacts the same disappearance. After genesis, it dissolves. It retains no keys, no votes, no capacity to intervene.

The founder's last act is their own removal.

5.3 — The One-Way Commitment

Because no private key exists, Bitcoin contributed to a jacket wallet cannot be recovered, redirected, or returned. Every contribution is permanent. The Bitcoin accumulates as a monument accumulates — through addition, never subtraction, over time.

The record is the accountability. There is no other mechanism of enforcement.

Part VI

Material Evolution

6.1 — The Principle of Accumulation

Each jacket moves in one direction: toward greater complexity and specificity. Patches are added. Marks accumulate. Repairs layer over wear. The jacket does not return to a prior state. This is the material proof of time. A jacket in its twentieth year is visibly, legibly different from a jacket in its first. The difference is not degradation. It is evidence.

6.2 — Patches

Patches may be added by any custodian, in any position, using any textile technique, with the following restrictions: no patch may cover the QR panel or any NFC button; no patch may incorporate commercial branding; no patch may be removed by a subsequent custodian.

The patch tradition draws directly from the waistcoat culture of motorcycle clubs, where the garment is a biography and status display simultaneously. In this system, patches carry no hierarchical meaning — their meaning is custodian-defined and recorded in the civic log.

6.3 — Wear and Repair

Normal wear is expected and welcome. A jacket that shows no wear has not been worn. A jacket with visible repairs has been maintained with care. Custodians are encouraged to repair rather than retire damaged sections. A visibly mended jacket carries more information than a pristine one.

6.4 — The Photographic Record

At each annual ceremony, and at each transfer, a full photographic record of each jacket is made and logged to the civic record. Over twenty years, these photographs constitute a document of twenty-one distinct lives — each jacket's slow divergence from its identical origin.

Part VII

Governance and Amendment

7.1 — Who Governs

The system is governed collectively by the active custodians of all twenty-one jackets. Each custodian holds one vote on matters of protocol. Decisions require a two-thirds majority.

The founding body dissolves permanently upon completion of the first transfer cycle. After genesis, no person or body holds authority over the system except its custodians. The system runs without its creator. This is the rule, not the outcome.

7.2 — What May Be Amended

The following may be amended by custodian vote: the location and structure of the annual ceremony; the transfer mechanism (though randomness must remain the basis of selection); the obligations of custodianship; the dashboard design and content.

The following are permanent and may not be amended:

  • The number of jackets: twenty-one
  • The Bitcoin-only value standard
  • The prohibition on ownership
  • The prohibition on audit trails for public interactions
  • The principle of material accumulation

7.3 — Dissolution

The system may not be dissolved. Individual jackets may be retired if destroyed beyond repair. The system continues as long as at least one jacket remains active and at least one custodian fulfils their obligations.

Section 1.7

The WORN Event

Public portrait record

Anyone who puts on the suit in a public context may add themselves to its permanent record. This is the WORN event — a chain entry consisting of a selfie photograph, a name, a location, and a confirmed act of consent. It is the only event type in the system that does not require custodian authentication. It is written by the subject.

The flow

  • The subject taps the right chest panel button while wearing the suit.
  • Their phone opens a dedicated capture page with the front-facing camera active.
  • They photograph themselves wearing the suit.
  • They enter their name and location.
  • They confirm, in plain language, that this photograph and their details will be added permanently to a public blockchain record and cannot be removed.
  • The submission enters a pending queue held by the node.
  • The custodian reviews and confirms the entry via the admin screen.
  • On confirmation, a signed WORN event is written to the chain. The photograph is stored on the node and replicated across the network. The entry is permanent.

What the record accumulates

The WORN event is designed for use at Bitcoin events, conferences, and public gatherings. Over time — across years, across custodians, across cities — the suit accumulates a portrait record of everyone who has worn it and chosen to be documented. Each entry is timestamped, chain-linked, and geographically located.

The record cannot be edited retroactively. It is what it was when it was made. In ten years, the portrait gallery of a well-travelled suit may contain people who were relatively unknown when photographed and who later became significant figures in the space. The chain does not revise its record of them.

The suit accumulates material evidence of time on its surface. The chain accumulates a portrait of everyone who wore it. Both records are permanent. Neither can be revised.

Consent and privacy

The WORN event is voluntary. The custodian does not photograph the subject — the subject photographs themselves. The consent field in the payload is a protocol record that the subject understood and agreed to the permanent, public nature of the entry before submitting. No record is created of people who decline.

"This is not wearable technology. It is a ceremonial interface for making decentralised value socially visible, materially embodied, and ritually accountable."

The jacket does not make digital wealth legible by explaining it. It makes it legible by wearing it — in public, once a year, in a square, in front of whoever happens to be there.

The scan is the audit. The wear is the proof. The transfer is the ceremony. The record is the accountability.
