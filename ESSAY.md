
# Abundance Money

A theory, a coin, and a different relationship with value


---

Most of us learn, without being taught it directly, that money is scarcity made portable. Gold is valuable because there is not much of it. A central bank's currency derives its value in part from controlling how many notes exist. Even our wages feel scarce — time is finite, energy is finite, and the money we earn in exchange for them seems to inherit that finitude. The anxiety many people feel about money is not irrational. It is a reasonable emotional response to a system that so often operates on the premise that there is never quite enough.

This premise is old and widespread. Questioning it can feel strange, even naive. And yet it is worth asking: is scarcity the only possible foundation for monetary value? Or is it simply the foundation we inherited, and kept, because for most of history we lacked the tools to build on anything else?


---

What scarcity money actually does

Scarcity money solves a real problem. If anyone could create money freely, money would quickly lose meaning. The constraint — limited gold, controlled issuance, fixed supply — is what prevents the unit of exchange from dissolving into worthlessness.

But the constraint carries costs that are not always named directly.

The first cost is psychological. A monetary system built on scarcity tends to train its participants to think in terms of accumulation and loss, holding and spending, enough and not enough. These are not inevitable human orientations. They are reinforced responses to an environment of enforced scarcity. The person who hoards, who defers gratification indefinitely, who treats every transaction as a potential loss — that person is behaving rationally within this system. But the system itself is a design choice, not a law of nature.

The second cost is material. Scarcity money has often relied on scarce materials to embody it — gold, silver, carefully controlled paper. The extraction of those materials has real consequences: mines, deforestation, carbon, displaced communities. The monetary system’s need for a scarce substrate is historically entangled with the environmental cost of obtaining it.

The third cost is temporal. Systems perceived as unstable or inflationary can encourage high time preference — the tendency to prioritise immediate value over long-term stewardship. If money may lose value, or if its supply can be altered by distant institutions, the rational response is often to spend or extract now rather than preserve for later. Much short-term economic thinking can be understood as an adaptation to these incentives rather than a personal failing.


---

The three abundances

Now consider three things that are genuinely, structurally abundant — not just plentiful for a time, but effectively inexhaustible on any human timescale.

Solar energy. The sun delivers more energy to the Earth's surface in one hour than human civilisation uses in a year. A photon that does not strike a solar panel simply warms the ground and disperses. At the point of origin, the supply is continuous and unconditional.

Plastic waste. This is abundance of a more troubling kind — the material legacy of a century of extractive manufacturing. Plastic waste exists in quantities that far exceed any realistic demand for raw material. It accumulates faster than it is addressed, and will remain with us for generations.

Human time on holiday. A person on holiday has, temporarily, stepped outside the productive economy. Their time is already allocated to leisure. The opportunity cost of two hours in a workshop is low — not because their time has no value, but because it would pass regardless, leaving no particular trace. This is a surplus that cannot be stored. It can only be used or allowed to disappear.

None of these has traditionally served as the basis of a monetary instrument. They are overlooked precisely because they are not scarce. But that raises a different question: what if value does not begin with scarcity, but with transformation?


---

Value from transformation, not from hoarding

Before scarcity-based money became dominant, many societies operated forms of gift economy. These were not simplistic or purely altruistic systems — they often contained hierarchy and obligation — but they demonstrate a different logic of value. In these systems, value was created and expressed through giving, through transformation, through attention directed toward others.

Status came not only from accumulation, but from the ability to take what was available — food, craft, materials — and turn it into something meaningful and shareable. What was scarce, in the deeper sense, was not the material itself, but the care, skill, and social relationship involved in its transformation.

Abundance money takes this principle seriously. It does not claim that scarcity is irrelevant. A stable system still requires a trustworthy reserve. That reserve may well be scarce.

But the instrument — the thing that changes hands, that carries the memory, that sits in the pocket or on the shelf — does not need to be made of something scarce. It can be made of something transformed.

The value is in the transformation. The material is the occasion for it.


---

The coin

A PlasticCoin is 40mm across, moulded from collected plastic waste and a small amount of ground olive stone from local groves. The energy that shaped it came from solar panels on a nearby roof. The person who made it may have spent a holiday afternoon in a farm workshop, shredding their own week’s rubbish and pressing it into this disc.

It is not scarce. The plastic was abundant and unwanted. The sunlight was passing through regardless. The time was already set aside.

And yet it holds real value — denominated in satoshis and backed one-to-one by a shared Bitcoin reserve. Each coin corresponds to an equivalent amount held in that reserve, which is publicly visible and auditable. The system is managed by a small group of mint operators, accountable to the community and operating under an open protocol.

Tap the small coloured disc set into the back and a page opens showing exactly what it is worth and exactly what backs it.

The coin makes four simple, verifiable claims:

The work is in the object — someone collected this material, ran the machine, and made the coin.

The energy is known — it was produced using solar power captured on site.

The reserve is visible — the Bitcoin backing can be checked by anyone at any time.

The origin is specific — this object comes from a particular place, at a particular moment, and carries that history in its surface.


---

Satoshis and the long horizon

Bitcoin is often described as a system of absolute scarcity — only twenty-one million coins will ever exist. This is true at the level of total supply. But at the scale of everyday use, something more subtle happens.

Each bitcoin can be divided into one hundred million satoshis. For a small community, satoshis are effectively non-scarce in daily exchange. The smallest denomination of a PlasticCoin represents only a tiny fraction of the total supply.

At the same time, the system that produces new bitcoin reduces its issuance over time. If its long-term trajectory holds, fewer new units will enter circulation while demand persists. The implication is simple: over long periods, the purchasing power of each unit may increase.

For a community holding reserves in this form, patience is structurally supported. Value does not need to be rushed into use. It can accumulate gradually through ordinary activity — small transactions, steady circulation, coins passed hand to hand.

This encourages a different temporal orientation. Not urgency, but continuity. Not extraction, but stewardship. The long view.


---

The totem

There is a word for an object that holds meaning beyond its material value, that connects the holder to a place and a memory, that can be passed from hand to hand carrying its history.

The word is totem.

Not token. Totem.

A token is interchangeable. One is as good as another. The individual object does not matter — only its denomination.

A totem is specific. This one, made on this day, from this material, by these hands. Its identity is inseparable from its history.

PlasticCoin is designed to be both. As money, it functions as a token — its denomination allows it to circulate. But as an object, it is a totem — uniquely formed, materially distinct, and tied to its origin.

A coin made on a summer afternoon can sit on a shelf for decades. It can be given as a gift, passed to a child, rediscovered years later. When tapped, it still verifies. It still holds its value. It still points back to the place it came from.

The satoshis it carries may change in purchasing power over time. The object itself does not. The material, the surface, the moment of its making — these remain.


---

The theory, stated simply

Abundance money is not the claim that scarcity does not matter. It is the claim that scarcity does not need to define the form of money.

When the instrument is made from abundant materials, transformed through attention rather than extracted at cost, the relationship to it changes. You are not holding a unit of enforced scarcity. You are holding the record of a transformation — something that was overlooked, gathered, and made meaningful.

That choice, repeated in small acts — in workshops, in markets, in everyday exchange — becomes visible over time. Value moving through a community. Materials given a second life. Energy used as it arrives. Trust accumulating slowly.

Abundance money is not only a theory. It is a practice.

It grows the way these things grow: gradually, locally, almost invisibly at first — like sunlight captured on a panel, like waste gathered before it disperses, like value held patiently in a satoshi over the long horizon.

The coin is small.

The argument it makes is long.
