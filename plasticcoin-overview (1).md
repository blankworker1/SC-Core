# PlasticCoin
## A new coin for a new economy 

---

There is a farm building at the edge of an Italian town. Olive trees have grown on this land for generations. On the roof of a nearby structure, solar panels catch the Mediterranean sun. Inside the building, a compact bench-top shredder and an injection moulding machine wait on a workbench. Beside them, a collection of plastic waste — bottles, caps, packaging — gathered by visitors during their stay in the town.

This is where the coin is made.

---

### The problem with money

Every form of money in human history has been built on the same idea: scarcity creates value. Gold is valuable because there is not much of it. A central bank's note is valuable because the bank controls how many are printed. Bitcoin is valuable in part because only twenty-one million will ever exist.

This logic is so old and so universal that we rarely question it. But it carries a hidden cost. When scarcity is the engine of value, the natural tendency is to extract: take the resource, deplete the reserve, discount the future. High time preference — the economic term for preferring gain now over sustainability later — is not just a personal failing. It is baked into the architecture of scarcity money.

PlasticCoin is an experiment in a different architecture. Not scarcity money. Abundance money.

---

### The material

Plastic waste is the most abundant discarded material on the planet. It is everywhere — on roadsides, in rivers, on beaches, accumulating in the margins of every human settlement. It has been extracted from oil, refined, manufactured into objects used briefly and then discarded. The extractive economy already made its pass. The entropy is already spent.

The coin intercepts this material on its way to oblivion.

The body of each PlasticCoin is injection-moulded from collected plastic waste — primarily the caps and containers that accumulate in a week of ordinary life. The energy used to melt and shape it comes entirely from solar panels: sunlight that would have warmed the ground regardless, captured and put to work. Two waste streams — discarded plastic and excess solar energy — combined in a simple machine to produce a durable object of value.

The coin also carries a small addition: coarsely ground olive stones from the local groves. A third waste stream, seasonal and place-specific, pressed into the material alongside the plastic. The olive harvest runs October through February. Coins made in that season carry the stone of that year's pressing. Coins made at other times carry stored stone from the last harvest. The material knows when and where it was made.

---

### The object

The finished coin is 40mm across — the size of a large old penny. It is a two-part object.

The body is moulded recycled plastic, unique in colour and texture to its batch. No two coins look the same, because no two collections of waste are the same. The surface carries a faint embossed mark — a recycling triangle whose negative space forms the letter A, for the first mint on the protocol. You can feel it with your thumb in the dark.

Set flush into the back is a small coloured disc, glued in place so that removing it would destroy the coin. The disc contains a chip smaller than a grain of rice. The colour of the disc is the denomination: red, blue, green, white, or black, in ascending value.

Tap the back of the coin against any modern phone and a page opens. It shows what the coin is worth, where it came from, and the proof that the value it represents is held in reserve. No app required. No account. No internet connection between the two people exchanging it. The value travels with the physical object, as it always did when people traded shells or passed a gold coin across a market table.

---

### The security

There is a deeper layer to this object that is invisible to the naked eye.

Recycled plastic is not a pure material. It is a mixture — many different source objects, different dye lots, different thermal histories, melted together and resolidified. The surface of every moulded coin carries a microstructure of extraordinary complexity: tiny ridges, voids, crystalline patterns, the frozen record of how that particular mixture cooled in that particular moment. No two surfaces are the same. No surface can be reproduced.

At the moment of manufacture, this surface is photographed and the pattern is enrolled in the ledger — a permanent, verifiable fingerprint. The chip carries the coin's identity. The surface confirms it. A counterfeiter who copied the chip would still need to reproduce the surface exactly — an impossibility, because the entropy that created it cannot be re-run. The disorder of the recycled material is the security mechanism.

This is worth pausing on. Industrial money fights impurity. Banknotes are printed on precisely specified paper with controlled fibres. Coins are struck from uniform metal alloys to exact tolerances. Purity is the goal because variation is the enemy of authentication.

PlasticCoin inverts this entirely. The impurity is the authentication. The variation is the point. The waste is the security.

---

### The four proofs

When you hold a PlasticCoin you are holding an object that makes four verifiable claims, each of them transparent and real.

**Proof of work.** Someone collected this plastic. Someone ran the machine. Someone enrolled the chip and the surface and entered the coin into the ledger. The labour is in the object, not hidden behind it.

**Proof of energy.** The manufacturing process ran on solar power. The electricity that shaped this coin came from the sun, not from burning stored carbon. The energy is captured in the object — as all energy in all manufactured things is, but here honestly and cleanly.

**Proof of reserves.** The value this coin represents is held in a Bitcoin reserve, publicly auditable by anyone at any time. The community dashboard shows the treasury. No one needs to trust the operator — they can check.

**Proof of visit.** For the tourist who made their coin in the farm workshop from their own week's waste, this object is a material record of being in this place at this time. Not a souvenir in the sense of a manufactured replica, but a direct physical transformation: their discarded plastic, their holiday, pressed into a durable coin. The ledger records the enrolment. The surface carries the fingerprint. The proof is in the object itself.

### The workshop

The first coins are made as a tourist activity. Visitors bring their week's plastic waste to the farm building. They watch — or take part in — the shredding, the melting, the moulding. Their own discarded packaging becomes the coin body. The coloured chip is pressed into the recess. The coin is photographed and enrolled.

The workshop is listed as a bookable experience — the kind of short, hands-on local activity that has become a familiar part of how people travel today. Platforms like Airbnb's Experiences section exist precisely for this: a few hours with someone who knows their craft, in a place that could not exist anywhere else. The coin-making workshop fits naturally into that category. You arrive with a bag of your own holiday rubbish. You leave with something made from it — something that is yours in a way no shop-bought souvenir could be.

For visitors who travel with Bitcoin, the workshop will in time be offered through newer platforms that operate entirely in that currency. The experience itself becomes a demonstration of what Bitcoin-native local commerce can look like: you pay in sats, you make a coin that holds sats, you carry it home. The loop is visible and complete within a single afternoon.

They leave with a sleeper: a coin with an identity but no active denomination yet. It sits dormant while the visitor travels home.

On launch day, the batch wakes. Every sleeper in the batch activates simultaneously. The tourist in Hamburg, in London, in Tokyo taps their coin. The page opens. The denomination is live. The value is real. The place reached out across distance and time.

This sequence is not a technical workaround. It is the story. The coin sleeps while the world turns. It wakes when the community decides it is ready.

### The products

The coin is the first thing made in the workshop, but not the only thing.

The same machines — the shredder and the injection moulder — can produce other objects from the same waste streams. The equipment is supplied by a UK company specialising in desktop-scale plastic recycling, whose machines are designed for exactly this kind of community and educational workshop context. Their standard mould range makes the product decisions straightforward: the workshop does not need to invent its product range from scratch, it needs to choose what belongs.

The choice of what to make is not arbitrary. Each product should belong to the place and serve a real need within it.

The second product is sunglasses. The community makes them through the winter months, when tourism is quiet and the workshop would otherwise sit idle. A production run through November, December, January — steady work for a small team, powered by the same solar panels that will run quieter in the lower winter sun but run nonetheless. By the time the beaches fill in June, there is stock. The sunglasses are sold at the beach, at the market, to the same visitors who arrive to make their souvenir coins. Waste plastic, made in the off-season, powered by the sun, worn in the sun. The seasonality is honest and the logic is tight.

The third product is flower pots. The workshop sits on agricultural land, in a town with a weekly market and a tradition of growing things. Small pots moulded from recycled plastic, filled with plants propagated on the farm, sold at the market stall alongside the produce. The pot is humble and functional — it makes no claims. But it carries the same material story as the coin: this was a bottle cap or a food container a week ago. Now it holds soil and a root. The transformation is visible to anyone who thinks about it for a moment, and invisible to anyone who does not. Both responses are fine.

These three products — coin, sunglasses, flower pot — share a workshop, a philosophy, and a waste stream. The market stall is the thread that connects them to the community directly: coins available for locals to hold, sunglasses for summer, plants and pots year-round. Together they begin to sketch what a small community economy built on transformation rather than extraction might look like in practice. Not a manifesto. Just a farm building, some machines, and the things that accumulate when people decide to pay attention to what they throw away.

### The economy

In circulation, the coin works simply. A resident buys food at the market and pays in coins. The merchant accepts them. Value moves without a bank, without a payment processor, without a fee extracted by a distant institution. At the end of the day the merchant can redeem the coins for their Bitcoin equivalent — or hold them and spend them in turn. The local economy has a closed loop that it controls.

The community treasury is public. Every resident can see how much is held in reserve, how many coins are in circulation, how recently the accounts were reconciled. This is not trustless — the operator is known, local, accountable. It is transparent. The community governs its own money and can see the books at any time.

When tourists buy coins as souvenirs and carry them home unredeemed, the value stays in the treasury. The town has effectively been paid for the plastic its visitors left behind, and for the solar energy used to transform it. External money flows in. Local value stays local.

---

### The deeper argument

Money is stored energy. This is true in every economy that has ever existed — money represents the human attention and physical work that produced it. But most money hides this. A euro tells you nothing about the energy that created it, the reserve backing it, or the hands it passed through. The opacity is a design choice. It makes money fungible, abstract, portable across any context.

PlasticCoin makes the opposite bet. Provenance adds value. A coin you can trace — materially, energetically, economically — is more meaningful than one you cannot. A community whose economy is fully legible to itself is more resilient than one that trusts distant institutions to manage the numbers on its behalf.

The coin is not trying to replace the euro. It is not making a claim about the entire global financial system. It is one small town, one farm building, one solar array, one press run of recycled plastic, saying: we know where this came from. We know what it cost. We know what backs it. We chose to make it this way.

That choice is the point. Entropy moves in one direction, and on a long enough timeline everything disperses. But the rate and manner of that dispersal is not fixed. You can burn the oil fast or you can catch the sunlight as it passes. You can discard the plastic or you can press it into something that holds value for years. You can build an economy on extraction or you can build one on transformation.

The coin is a small, holdable argument that the second path exists.

---

*PlasticCoin is an open protocol. The first mint is in Italy. The second can be anywhere there is plastic, sunlight, and a community that wants to see its economy clearly.*
